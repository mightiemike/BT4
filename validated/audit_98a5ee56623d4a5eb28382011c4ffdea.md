### Title
Deposit Refund Misdirection in Meta Transactions Enables Relayer Fund Theft — (File: `runtime/runtime/src/lib.rs`)

---

### Summary

In NEAR's meta transaction (NEP-366/`DelegateAction`) implementation, when an inner action carrying an attached deposit fails on the receiver's shard, the protocol sends the deposit refund to the **predecessor of the inner receipt** (the user, Alice) rather than to the **relayer who actually paid for the deposit**. An unprivileged user can deliberately craft a meta transaction whose inner action will fail on the receiver's shard, causing the relayer to permanently lose the deposit to the user.

---

### Finding Description

The NEAR runtime's deposit-refund invariant states: *"Deposit refunds are generated when an action receipt fails to execute. All attached deposit amounts are summed together and sent as a refund to a `predecessor_id`."* [1](#0-0) 

In a normal (non-meta) transaction the predecessor of the action receipt is the signer, who also paid for the deposit, so the invariant is consistent. In a meta transaction the payment chain diverges from the predecessor chain:

1. **Relayer** submits `SignedTransaction` → Alice's shard. The relayer's balance is debited for **all** gas and **all** deposits inside the `DelegateAction`.
2. Alice's shard unpacks the `DelegateAction` and emits a new inner `ActionReceipt` to Bob's shard. The `predecessor_id` of this inner receipt is **Alice** (not the relayer).
3. If the inner action fails on Bob's shard, `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` computes `deposit_refund = total_deposit` and calls `Receipt::new_balance_refund(receipt.predecessor_id(), deposit_refund)`. [2](#0-1) [3](#0-2) 

The `receiver_id` of the resulting refund receipt is **Alice's account ID** — the predecessor of the failed inner receipt — even though the relayer's balance was debited. The relayer receives no deposit refund.

The protocol documentation explicitly acknowledges this divergence:

> *"But what if the transaction fails execution on Bob's shard? At this point, the predecessor is `Alice` and therefore she receives the token balance refunded, not the relayer. This is something relayer implementations must be aware of since there is a financial incentive for Alice to submit meta transactions that have high balances attached but will fail on Bob's shard."* [4](#0-3) 

The **exact divergent value** is the `receiver_id` field of the `new_balance_refund` receipt: it is set to `receipt.predecessor_id()` (Alice) instead of the relayer's account ID.

---

### Impact Explanation

A malicious user (Alice) can drain a relayer's NEAR balance by repeatedly submitting `SignedDelegateAction` objects that:
- Attach a large deposit to an inner `FunctionCall` or `Transfer` action.
- Target a receiver (Bob) where the action is guaranteed to fail (e.g., a non-existent method, a contract that always panics, or an account that does not exist).

Each such meta transaction causes the relayer to lose the full attached deposit to Alice. Because the relayer pays upfront and the refund is routed to Alice at the protocol level, no off-chain check by the relayer can prevent the loss once the transaction is submitted. The relayer's only mitigation is to refuse to relay any meta transaction that carries a deposit — which defeats the purpose of the feature.

**Severity: High** — direct, repeatable, unprivileged financial loss from the relayer to the attacker.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- The attack requires no special privilege: any account that can create a `DelegateAction` can exploit this.
- The `DelegateAction` is a stable, production feature (NEP-366, protocol version 53+).
- The attacker controls the inner actions and their deposits entirely; the relayer cannot inspect whether an inner action will succeed before submitting.
- Off-chain simulation cannot reliably predict on-chain failure (contract state can change between simulation and execution), mirroring the hook-contract divergence in the original report. [5](#0-4) 

---

### Recommendation

1. **Track the original deposit payer in the receipt.** The `ActionReceiptV2` struct already has a `refund_to` field. Populate it with the relayer's account ID when a `DelegateAction` is unwrapped, so that deposit refunds from inner-action failures are routed to the relayer rather than to Alice. [6](#0-5) 

2. **Protocol change:** Modify `refund_unspent_gas_and_deposits` to use `action_receipt.refund_to()` (when set) as the deposit-refund destination instead of `receipt.predecessor_id()`. [7](#0-6) 

3. Until a protocol fix lands, relayer implementations should refuse to relay any `DelegateAction` that carries a non-zero deposit on any inner action, or require the user to pre-fund an escrow that the relayer controls.

---

  }

Relayer wraps it in a SignedTransaction (relayer is signer).
  → relayer's balance is debited 10 NEAR (deposit) + gas costs.

On Alice's shard:
  DelegateAction is verified and unwrapped.
  Inner ActionReceipt is emitted to victim-contract.near.
  predecessor_id of inner receipt = alice.near.

On victim-contract.near's shard:
  FunctionCall executes → panics → receipt fails.
  deposit_refund = 10 NEAR.
  new_balance_refund(receiver_id = alice.near, refund = 10 NEAR) is created.

Result:
  Relayer lost 10 NEAR (deposit) + gas costs.
  Alice gained 10 NEAR.
  Net transfer: 10 NEAR from relayer to Alice per iteration.

## Relayer

Meta transactions only work with a relayer. This is an application layer
concept, implemented off-chain. Think of it as a server that accepts a
`SignedDelegateAction`, does some checks on them and eventually forwards it
inside a transaction to the blockchain network.

A relayer may choose to offer their service for free but that's not going to be
financially viable long-term. But they could easily have the user pay using
other means, outside of Near blockchain. And with some tricks, it can even be
paid using fungible tokens on Near.

In the example visualized above, the payment is done using \$FT. Together with
the transfer to John, Alice also adds an action to pay 0.1 \$FT to the relayer.
The relayer checks the content of the `SignedDelegateAction` and only processes
it if this payment is included as the first action. In this way, the relayer
will be paid in the same transaction as John.

Note that the payment to the relayer is still not guaranteed. It could be that
Alice does not have sufficient $FT and the transfer fails. To mitigate, the
relayer should check the $FT balance of Alice first.

Unfortunately, this still does not guarantee that the balance will be high
enough once the meta transaction executes. The relayer could waste NEAR gas
without compensation if Alice somehow reduces her \$FT balance in just the right
moment. Some level of trust between the relayer and its user is therefore
required.

The vision here is that there will be mostly application-specific relayers. A
general-purpose relayer is difficult to implement with just the MVP. See
limitations below.
